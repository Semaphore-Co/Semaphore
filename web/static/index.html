Frequently Asked Questions

Your question is likely answered in the whitepaper.

  • How is Semaphore more than "Tinder with a blockchain"?
    • Semaphore has fundamentally different goals than traditional dating apps. Its scope goes beyond romantic interest, but even within the realm of matching romantic partners, Semaphore is about discovering hidden connections rather than pairing up strangers. Tinder matches involve very little commitment, and many users find their time wasted with frivolous pairings. Semaphore is for people who already know each other and want to determine whether a feeling (such as a crush) is mutual.
  • Why require tokens for matches when it could be done for free?
    • Requiring tokens adds a cost to Semaphore matches, which is the only effective way to demonstrate that the message is genuine. Without this kind of commitment, users could signal everything to everyone just to see what responses they get, and the whole system becomes unreliable. From a user's perspective, spending a small amount of money will ensure that any response is genuine, a service you can't get anywhere else.
  • Why would the average person have any interest in Semaphore?
    • Even if nothing else about the platform interests someone, there's always the lure of free money. A key feature of Semaphore is that users anonymously receive the tokens that other people commit when matching with them. Someone who is popular, well-connected, or attractive has a chance to earn money by signing up for Semaphore and evangelizing it to their peers. As a result, Semaphore markets itself.
  • Doesn't Semaphore rely too heavily on "network effects" to be useful?
    • Not particularly. A user wishing to signal to someone without a Semaphore account provides some of that person's contact information, and they receive an explanation of what Semaphore is and instructions on signing up. The tokens they can claim, worth real money, provide an incentive to sign up and see what Semaphore is about. Users won't even know who else is using Semaphore.
  • Why is blockchain technology useful for this application?
    • As a distributed application, Semaphore is trustless. There is no private data stored in a central database. Instead, match information is stored on the Ethereum blockchain, with the potential for further obfuscation using zk-SNARKs. As countless hacks (such as Ashley Madison in 2015) have demonstrated, centralized databases are a liability when it comes to this kind of information.
  • Who's developing Semaphore?
    • Semaphore was imagined and designed by Dennis Maloney, a former Computer Science and Economics student at MIT. Semaphore is currently a two-man project between Dennis and his business partner Kyle Corbin. We are eager to expand the Semaphore team as necessary to achieve the vision laid out in the whitepaper.
  • What does the name mean?
    • A semaphore is a signal, and Semaphore is a platform for sending reliable signals. Additionally, "semaphore" has a technical meaning within the realm of computer science. Computer programs are often split up into separate threads that run at the same time. A semaphore is a variable used to make sure that different threads don't access and change a common resource at the same time, which would cause problems in the program. Semaphore serves an analogous role: it controls access to information in a manner that ensures no one gets hurt.
